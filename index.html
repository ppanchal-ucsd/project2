Project #2 – Persuasion & Counter‑Persuasion

Author: Pooja Panchal · Date: Oct 25, 2025

Proposition: YES: States that lost abortion clinics between 2017 and 2020 experienced significant declines in abortion rates. NO: Losing abortion clinics does not necessarily lead to lower abortion rates — in some states, rates even increased despite clinic closures.

Source: Guttmacher Institute (2017–2020), rates per 1,000 women 15–44.

Visualization A — Argues the proposition is True

Design Decisions (with scores)

  • +1.0 Added linear trend line – earnest summary of overall relationship.
  • -0.5 Truncated axes to ±100% – slightly exaggerates slope.
  • -1.0 Highlighted decline states – focuses attention on supportive evidence.
  • +0.5 Annotated one contradictory case – improves credibility.

Overall tone: Slightly earnest (~0.0).

Visualization B — Argues the proposition is False

Design Decisions (with scores)

  • +0.5 Sorted bars descending – improves readability.
  • -1.0 Cropped x-axis to -20% – magnifies positive changes.
  • -1.0 Colored rising states brightly; others gray – emphasizes supporting evidence.
  • +0.5 Added 3 annotations – helps identify key examples.

Overall tone: Slightly deceptive (~-0.5).

Reflection

Making two different visualizations from the same dataset helped me understand how much design choices can shape a story. It was pretty easy to make charts that looked nice, but harder to make them persuasive without crossing into misleading territory. I was surprised by how small changes-like axis limits or color could completely change how someone reads the data. For the “YES” chart, I focused on showing that clinic losses led to lower abortion rates. That part felt straightforward, but I realized that even an honest-looking trend line can subtly push a message. For the “NO” chart, it was trickier because I had to make the opposite point. I used color and sorting to draw attention to states where abortion rates rose even with fewer clinics. That chart looked convincing, but I knew it left out some context, so I gave it a slightly deceptive score. This project made me think more about the ethics of data visualization. I now see ethical design as being persuasive while still letting the viewer find the truth if they look carefully. A visualization crosses the line when it hides or distorts the data to support one side. My goal going forward is to design visuals that inform first, and persuade second-so that people can trust what they see.
